**ABSTRACT**

17β-estradiol (E2) regulates the activity of the gonadotropin-releasing hormone (GnRH) neurons through both presynaptic and postsynaptic mechanisms, and this ovarian steroid hormone is essential for cyclical GnRH neuronal activity and secretion. E2 has significant actions to modulate the mRNA expression of numerous ion channels in GnRH neurons and/or to enhance (suppress) endogenous conductances (currents) including potassium ($K_{\text{ATP}}$, A-type) and calcium low voltage T-type and high voltage L-type currents. Also, it is well documented that E2 can alter the excitability of GnRH neurons via direct action, but the intracellular signaling cascades mediating these actions are not well understood. As an example, $K_{\text{ATP}}$ channels are critical ion channels needed for maintaining GnRH neurons in a hyperpolarized state for recruiting T-type calcium channels that are important for burst firing in GnRH neurons. E2 modulates the activity of $K_{\text{ATP}}$ channels via a membrane-initiated signaling pathway in GnRH neurons. Obviously there are other channels, including the small conductance activated $K^+$ (SK) channels, that maybe modulated by this signaling pathway, but the ensemble of mER-, ERα-, and ERβ-mediated effects both pre- and postsynaptic will ultimately dictate the excitability of GnRH neurons.

4. Multiple estrogen receptors and signaling cascades are involved in 17β-estradiol modulation of GnRH neuronal activity

Because ERα, the first cloned receptor/transcription factor for E2, has not been localized to native GnRH neurons, the predominant view has been that estrogen affects GnRH neurons exclusively through pre-synaptic mechanisms as outlined above. However, a second ER, ERβ, is expressed in GnRH neurons (Herbison and Pape, 2001; Hrabovszky et al., 2001; Kallo et al., 2001) and a number of studies have illustrated that the acute actions of E2 on GnRH neurons are ERβ-dependent (see below). Still, the functional significance of ERβ in GnRH neurons remains uncertain. In addition, synaptically isolated GnRH neurons are rapidly hyperpolarized by E2, an effect that inhibits their firing (Kelly et al., 1984; Condon et al., 1989; Lagrange et al., 1995). It is thought that these hyperpolarizing actions of E2 on GnRH neurons are via a G protein (Gαi/o)-coupled receptor. Indeed, in GT1-7 cells, an immortalized GnRH neuronal cell line, E2 inhibits adenylyl cyclase activity (cAMP production) via a pertussis toxin-sensitive Gαi/o coupling mechanism (Navarro et al., 2003). Because of the rapid onset of these E2 effects the involvement of transcription is highly unlikely, but an E2-responsive Gαi/o-coupled receptor has not been identified. E2 can also act acutely to increase calcium oscillations in primate GnRH neurons via G protein-receptor 30 (GPR30) (Terasawa et al., 2010).

The observation that E2 treatment in vivo can within 15 min induce phosphorylation of cyclic AMP response element binding protein (pCREB) in GnRH neurons, was a clear indication of E2 rapid signaling within the hypothalamus. Moreover, this effect was lost in animals with a deletion mutation of ERβ, but not ERα, suggesting a role for ERβ in the E2-induced rapid induction of pCREB in mouse GnRH neurons (Abraham et al., 2003). A role for ERβ has been further supported by studies using whole-cell recordings of GnRH neurons to illustrate that high physiological levels (0.1–100 nM) of E2 and the ERβ agonist diarylpropionitrile (DPN) both of which act acutely (within 15 min) to enhance action potential firing, reduce the afterhyperpolarization potential (AHP) and increase the amplitude of a slow afterdepolarization (ADP) in GnRH neurons (Chu et al., 2009). These effects were observed in the presence of ionotropic GABA and glutamate blockade (although other inputs such as kisspeptin were not blocked), suggesting direct, rapid excitatory actions by E2 on GnRH neurons that involve ERβ. Other rapid actions of E2 to alter calcium transients in GnRH neurons are thought to involve modulation of presynaptic GABA input and appear to be dependent on ERα. Thus physiological levels (1–100 nM) of E2 (within 15 min) can increase calcium transients in GnRH neurons via an ERα-dependent release of GABA from presynaptic terminals (Romanò et al., 2008). However, E2 (10 pM) may also act indirectly to rapidly inhibit GnRH neurons in an ERα-dependant manner (Chu et al., 2009). Therefore, the cumulative findings would suggest rapid presynaptic actions of E2 via ERα and rapid postsynaptic actions via ERβ to either excite or inhibit GnRH neurons.

The ability of E2 to acutely modify GnRH neurons through calcium signaling has been further explored in mouse hypothalamic neuronal explants and in primate nasal placode cultures. In these models, E2 (0.2–1 nM) augments synchronous intracellular Ca²⁺ oscillations in GnRH neurons (Temple et al., 2004; Temple and Wray, 2005; Abe et al., 2008). This relatively rapid effect is observed within 10–30 min of E2 application. The calcium oscillations in the presence of TTX are most likely the result of E2 acting directly on GnRH neurons. Furthermore, E2 conjugated to BSA at the C-17 position (E2-17-BSA) or to a large non-degradable poly(amido)amine-dendrimer macromolecule (EDC) (Harrington et al., 2006) mimics the effects of E2 on intracellular calcium oscillations (Temple et al., 2004; Temple and Wray, 2005; Abe et al., 2008). In the mouse explants experiments, the E2-induced calcium oscillations are blocked by ICI 182,780 suggesting that an ER is involved (Temple et al., 2004; Temple and Wray, 2005). The actions of E2-BSA in GnRH neuronal cultures are abrogated by pertussis toxin treatment supporting a role for a G protein-coupled membrane receptor (Temple et al., 2004; Temple and Wray, 2005; Noel et al., 2009). In mouse, the E2-induced calcium oscillations are observed in 7 division explants which express ERβ, but not in 21 division explants which lack ERβ. Therefore, it is hypothesized that these rapid membrane actions in mouse GnRH neurons are ERβ-dependent (Temple et al., 2004). In contrast, E2-induced calcium oscillations in the primate placode cultures are not blocked by the antagonist ICI 182,780, and ICI 182,780 alone has no effect (Abe et al., 2008; Noel et al., 2009).

Interestingly, GPR30 is expressed in primate GnRH neurons and may, in part, be responsible for the rapid membrane-initiated actions of E2 in these neurons (Noel et al., 2009). Thus E2 conjugated EDC failed to induce calcium oscillations in cultured GnRH neurons transiently transfected with human GPR30 siRNA, but not with the control siRNA, further supporting a role for GPR30 (Noel et al., 2009). GPR30 has received considerable attention because it exhibits binding and signaling characteristics of a membrane ER (Revankar et al., 2005; Thomas et al., 2005; Filardo and Thomas, 2005; Funakoshi et al., 2006; Matsuda et al., 2008). Although there is some controversy whether this receptor is expressed in the plasma membrane or localized to the endoplasmic reticulum (Revankar et al., 2005; Thomas et al., 2005; Filardo and Thomas, 2005; Funakoshi et al., 2006; Matsuda et al., 2008), there is general agreement on the estrogen-mediated signaling. For example in breast cancer cells that are transfected with GPR30, estrogen activates the MAP kinases, ERK1 and ERK2 (Filardo, 2002; Filardo and Thomas, 2005). In addition, E2 can also stimulate adenylyl cyclase activity, which leads to PKA-mediated suppression of epidermal growth factor (EGF)-induced ERK1/2 activity. However, the E2-induced GPR30 signaling pathway in GnRH neurons is currently unknown (Terasawa et al., 2010). Collectively, these data suggest a rapid direct action of E2 through ERβ and perhaps GPR 30 to excite GnRH neurons and a rapid indirect action via ERα to excite or suppress GnRH neuronal excitability. How these acute actions contribute to the overall physiology of GnRH neurons is currently not well understood.

Genetic models have been generated from ERα knockout (αERKO) animals that possess an ER-knock-in mutation, which allows in vivo distinction between estrogen response element (ERE)-dependent and ERE-independent mechanisms of E2 action (McDevitt et al., 2008; Glidewell-Kenney et al., 2008). This ERE-independent model was used to study GnRH neurons,
using loose cell attached recordings (Christian et al., 2008). Based on these studies it was concluded that an E2-induced decrease in GnRH neuronal firing during the morning (negative feedback model) as well as increased neuronal firing during the afternoon (positive feedback model) are both dependent on ERα binding to ERE. In contrast, based on measurements of E2-induced inhibition of plasma LH in ovariectomized wildtype (WT), αERKO and ER-knock-in mutant animals (E207A/G208A; “AA”), negative feedback regulation of LH (GnRH) is at least in part dependent on a mechanism other than ER binding to ERE (McDevitt et al., 2008). Similarly, the E2 inhibition of arcuate kisspeptin neurons, believed to mediate negative feedback, is also via an ERE-independent mechanism (Gottsch et al., 2009). These data would indicate that feedback regulation of E2 to inhibit or excite GnRH neurons is quite complex involving multiple receptors and both pre- and post-synaptic E2 actions, requiring further experiments for a more complete understanding.

It is well known that Kir channels are critical for maintaining excitable cells in a hyperpolarized resting state closer to E_K, but once cells are depolarized the channels allow for the quick transition to long depolarizing responses because of their inwardly rectifying properties (Hille, 2001). With greater membrane hyperpolarization of GnRH neurons there is an increase in the number of Na⁺ spikes during rebound excitation (Zhang et al., 2009a). Indeed, K_ATP and GIRK channels appear to be the critical Kir channels in GnRH neurons for maintaining the membrane in a relatively negative resting state (Zhang et al., 2007, 2009b). Blocking K_ATP channels with the sulfonylurea tolbutamide significantly depolarizes GnRH neurons (Fig. 1), which is indicative of tonic K_ATP channel activity that is significantly augmented with E2 treatment (Zhang et al., 2007). We also know that GABAergic, opioid, neuropeptide Y and perhaps melanin-concentrating hormone (MCH) input via their respective GABA_B, μ-opioid, Y5 and MCH1 receptors activates GIRK channels to hyperpolarize GnRH neurons (Lagrange et al., 1995; Zhang et al., 2009b; Xu et al., 2009; Wu et al., 2009). This combination of membrane hyperpolarization would allow for the recruitment of multiple excitatory conductances that are critical for generating burst firing in CNS neurons (Huguenard and McCormick, 1992; Erickson et al., 1993; Kelly and Wagner, 2002).

Recent findings have revealed that GnRH neurons express ATP-sensitive K⁺ (K_ATP) channels and the agonist-induced outward (K_ATP) current is increased by approximately two-fold in E2-treated animals (Zhang et al., 2007). The increased channel activity, which occurs in the presence of TTX and GABA and glutamate blockade, is not the result of increased expression of the channel based on quantitative real-time PCR results from harvested GnRH neurons. Therefore, it appears that there is an E2-induced change in signaling molecules (kinases) that impinge on the channel, part of which may be due to acute phosphorylation events that non-the-less are long acting effects on cell function. Indeed, our recent findings suggest that E2 may signal via a mER to hyperpolarize GnRH neurons, as defined by the mER ligand STX and the inhibition of the response by ICI 182,780 (Qiu et al., 2003, 2006; Zhang et al., 2010). Previously, we found that E2 could hyperpolarize guinea pig GnRH neurons via activation of a K⁺ channel and hypothesized that this could contribute to the negative feedback actions of the hormone (Lagrange et al., 1995). In our original studies we found that E2 activated an inwardly rectifying K⁺ (Kir) channel (Lagrange et al., 1995). In mouse GnRH neurons, we do not see a change in the holding current (indicative of a direct K⁺ channel activation) but rather an augmentation of the K_ATP channel opener (diazoxide)-activated current. The apparent direct activation of Kir channels in the guinea pig versus the augmentation of a Kir channel activity in the mouse is most likely due to the different recording conditions, sharp electrode recordings in the guinea pig versus the whole-cell patch recordings (whole-cell dialysis) in the mouse GnRH neurons. Alternatively, it could reflect a subtle species difference. Regardless, in both species E2 acutely augments a Kir current that results in a membrane hyperpolarization, which critically sets the stage for recruiting “excitatory” channels (see below). In addition, there are neurotransmitters (GABA and opioids) that activate G protein-activated inwardly rectifying K⁺ (GIRK) channels (Lagrange et al., 1995; Zhang et al., 2009b) and these channels will also contribute to the membrane hyperpolarization (see the Kelly and Qiu chapter, this volume).

In addition, it has been known for some time that the peptide GnRH functions by a short-circuit feedback pathway to modulate the excitability of GnRH neurons and reduce GnRH secretion (Padmanabhan et al., 1995; Krsmanic et al., 1999; Xu et al., 2004; Herbison, 2006). Indeed, GnRH activates the M-current in a sub-population of GnRH neurons (Xu et al., 2004, 2008). The receptor mediating this effect is the GnRH receptor 1 (GnRH-R1) (Xu et al., 2004, 2008; Todman et al., 2005). In TTX, which blocks synaptic input, the activation of this persistent K⁺ current by GnRH causes a hyperpolarization (Xu et al., 2008; Han et al., 2010) that along with activation of other K⁺ currents would contribute to the inhibition of cell firing. GnRH has also been found to depolarize GnRH neurons and to act transsynaptically to regulate the activity of GnRH neurons via modulation of GABAergic input to these cells (Todman et al., 2005; Chen and Moenter, 2009; Han et al., 2010). In immortalized GnRH neurons, GnRH in nanomolar concentrations activates GnRH-R1, which results in Gq/11 activation of PLC signaling along with coupling to Gs and increased cAMP production (Krsmanic et al., 2003). However, the complete signaling mechanism in native GnRH neurons is not known.

Studies in eGFP-GnRH mice have illustrated that GnRH neurons often fire in episodes of repetitive bursts (Fig. 1) (Suter et al., 2000b; Kuehl-Kovarik et al., 2002, 2005). Currently, it is not known how the bursts are generated in GnRH neurons, although depolarizing afterpotentials (DAP; also called after-depolarizing potentials, ADP) are proposed to be involved (Kuehl-Kovarik et al., 2005; Chu and Moenter, 2006). Based on current clamp and voltage clamp recordings in neurosecretory hypothalamic neurons, models have been developed that include an ensemble of channels that appear to be critical for burst firing in parvocellular neurosecretory neurons (Kelly and Rønnekleiv, 1994; Kelly and Wagner, 2002; Lee et al., 2010). Based on these models, calcium T-type channels together with the hyperpolarization-activated cation current (I_h) and calcium-dependent small (SK)-type channels (underlying an AHP) are crucial for burst firing.

Therefore, the E2-dependent increase in T-type calcium channels may serve to augment the excitability of GnRH neurons in preparation for the GnRH (LH) surge. Not surprisingly, L-type calcium currents are also regulated by E2 in GnRH neurons ([Sun et al., 2010](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.yfrne.2010.01.001)). In ovariectomized mice treated with E2 to induce afternoon LH surges, the peak L-type current was reduced during the morning at the time of negative feedback but increased during the afternoon at the time of positive feedback. This would indicate that L-type currents may be significant contributors to the afternoon surge of GnRH (LH). However, the diurnal signal that precisely activates this current is unknown. In addition, GnRH neurons express a time-dependent, hyperpolarization-activated, cation current (*I*ₕ or pacemaker current) that contributes to rhythmic firing ([Lagrange et al., 1995](https://doi.org/10.1016/0306-4522(95)00316-8); [Kelly and Wagner, 2002](https://doi.org/10.1016/S0306-4522(02)00002-0); [Zhang et al., 2007](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.yfrne.2007.02.002)).

Neuronal excitability is also determined by the after-hyperpolarization (AHP) that follows an action potential, and SK channels underlie a component of the median after-hyperpolarization (mAHP) K⁺ currents in CA1 pyramidal cells and other neurons ([Bond et al., 2004](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.neuron.2004.03.016)). SK channel subunits are expressed in GnRH neurons ([Bosch et al., 2002](https://doi.org/10.1016/S0896-6273(02)00620-5); [Kato et al., 2006](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.yfrne.2006.01.001)), and SK channel activation exerts a powerful influence on firing properties of GnRH neurons. In particular, these channels and other AHP channels appear to regulate both intraburst and interburst neuronal firing in GnRH neurons and may play a key role in modulating GnRH neuronal excitability ([Kato et al., 2006](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.yfrne.2006.01.001); [Liu and Herbison, 2008](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.yfrne.2008.01.001); [Lee et al., 2010](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.yfrne.2010.01.002)).

Finally, Kir channels are negatively modulated by a number of Gq-coupled receptors including the kisspeptin receptor GPR 54 in GnRH neurons, which allows the rapid transition to heightened excitatory states ([Kobrinsky et al., 2000](https://doi.org/10.1016/S0896-6273(00)80946-0); [Hille, 2001](https://doi.org/10.1016/S0896-6273(01)00266-0); [Liu et al., 2008](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.yfrne.2008.01.002); [Zhang et al., 2008](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.yfrne.2008.01.003)). Kisspeptin potently depolarizes hypothalamic GnRH neurons through a combination of inhibiting an inwardly rectifying potassium (Kir) conductance and activating a transient receptor potential canonical (TRPC) conductance ([Zhang et al., 2008](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.yfrne.2008.01.003); [Liu et al., 2008](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.yfrne.2008.01.002); [Pielecka-Fortuna et al., 2008](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.yfrne.2008.01.004)). To date, kisspeptin is the most potent and efficacious neuropeptide/neurotransmitter to modulate GnRH neuronal activity ([Lagrange et al., 1995](https://doi.org/10.1016/0306-4522(95)00316-8); [Spergel et al., 1999](https://doi.org/10.1016/S0306-4522(99)00316-8); [Kuehl-Kovarik et al., 2002](https://doi.org/10.1016/S0306-4522(02)00316-8), [2005](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.yfrne.2005.01.001); [DeFazio et al., 2002](https://doi.org/10.1016/S0306-4522(02)00316-8); [Suter, 2004](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.yfrne.2004.01.001); [Han et al., 2002](https://doi.org/10.1016/S0306-4522(02)00316-8); [Herbison, 2006](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.yfrne.2006.01.001); [Zhang et al., 2008](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.yfrne.2008.01.003)). Besides activating TRPC channels, kisspeptin also attenuates a resting (barium and cesium sensitive) Kir current in GnRH neurons ([Fig. 2](#fig2)). The inhibition of Kir maybe critical because Kir channels (e.g., *K*ₐₜₚ channels) are highly expressed in GnRH neurons and clamp the cells in a negative resting state of –63 mV ([Lagrange et al., 1995](https://doi.org/10.1016/0306-4522(95)00316-8); [Zhang et al., 2007](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.yfrne.2007.02.002)).

2007, 2009b). This effect of kisspeptin would also be vital for inhibiting GPCR-activated (μ-opioid, GABA<sub>B</sub> and MCH) Kir currents which are active in GnRH neurons (Lagrange et al., 1995; Zhang et al., 2009b; Wu et al., 2009). Therefore by inhibiting Kir channels along with the pronounced activation of TRPC channels, kisspeptin depolarizes the cells to threshold (~–45 mV) and induces sustained firing. Interestingly, GnRH neurons express all of the “brain” TRPC channel subunits (TRPC1,3,4,5,6,7) with TRPC1, 4 and 5 being the most prevalent in GnRH neurons (Zhang et al., 2008). Based on the current-voltage relationship, pharmacological profile and mRNA expression, TRPC1, 4, and 5 are key players in mediating the effects of kisspeptin in GnRH neurons. Moreover, the kisspeptin receptor GPR54 couples to a Gq and phospholipase C (PLC) pathway in GnRH neurons (Zhang et al., 2008; Liu et al., 2008; Constantin et al., 2009), and kisspeptin activation of this pathway results in the release of calcium from intracellular stores after stimulation of inositol-1,4,5-trisphosphate (IP3) receptors, which would activate the store-operated TRPC1,4 and 5 channels in GnRH neurons (Fig. 2) (Liu et al., 2008).
